Dr. Ramón Enrique Gaviola

Here's a time line of Dr. Ramón Enrique Gaviola:

  • 1900 - Born in Rivadavia, province of Mendoza.

  • 1917 - He began his training at the Faculty of Engineering of La Plata.

  • 1922 - Following the advice of one of his professors, Richard Gans, after graduating as a surveyor in order to obtain some money to pay for his studies and the trip, he enrolled at the University of Göttingen, Germany, where he began his studies in physics and would be a student. of the most distinguished scientists of the time and the century.

  • 1926 - His graduation thesis, supervised by Max von Laue and Walter Nernst, obtained an outstanding grade "Magna cum laude" and on June 6 of this same year, he attended the ritual graduation ceremony as Philosophiae Doctoris et Artium Liberalium Magistri, of the Friedrich Wilhelms University of Berlin.

    Upon finishing his studies in Germany, Einstein suggested that he apply for a Rockefeller Fellowship to the International Education Board to go to work in Baltimore in the laboratory of the great American physicist Robert Williams Wood. He came first in the order of merits, but the scholarship was denied, as it was not planned to be awarded to someone who did not come from the US or Europe. Such a situation provoked Einstein's anger, "I showed him that [the rejection note] Einstein -Gaviola would remember- and Einstein got angry, and right there, on the edge of the stairs, he wrote a letter to the Rockefeller representative." It was the first case to be awarded to a candidate from the southern hemisphere.

  • 1928 - After completing the work with Wood, he moved to Washington where the work he carried out during the years 1928 and 1929 was notable.

    Until mid-1929 he served at that Institution as an assistant physicist in the Department of Terrestrial Magnetism. Along with Merle Tuve and Harry Lawrence Hafstad, at the Carnegie Institution, working on vacuum and high-voltage techniques. He also published a series of works of which Noteworthy: Gaviola E. An Experimental Test of Schrödinger's Theory. Nature. Nov. 17, 1928, (constituted the first experimental work on stimulated atomic emission. The origin of the current laser.

  • 1929 - In 1929, in Physical Review: "On time lags in fluorescence and in the Kerr and Fadaray effects". Experimental work that contributed to give rise to two new scientific areas: fluorescent spectrometry in biochemistry and the study of protein hydrodynamics. The fluorometer he designed and built to do so is known today by his name.

  • 1930 - He returned to Argentina to work at the University of Buenos Aires, where he revolutionized study methods and gave great impetus to experimental work. He also managed to teach electromagnetism, radiation thermodynamics, kinetic theory and quantum theory for the first time, which until now had not been incorporated into the curriculum. He also began a preaching for the scientific development of the country and held important positions, such as Director of the Astronomical Observatory of Córdoba, and professor at several universities, such as that of Buenos Aires, where he directed the Chair of Physics-Chemistry at the Faculty of Sciences. Exact, Physical and Natural (1930-1936). He also promoted the creation of the Argentine Physical Association, which he would chair, and the Institute of Mathematics, Astronomy and Physics of the Astronomical Observatory of Córdoba created in 1956 to support the activities of the Observatory.

  • 1931 - He receives a letter from Max Born proposing to send Georg Rumer, his main collaborator, to work with him. Unfortunately, the proposal raised by Gaviola for the appointment was not accepted and Argentina lost an excellent opportunity for the development of Theoretical Physics in the country.

  • 1935 - The situation of the Astronomical Observatory of Córdoba was in a critical situation and its closure was even mentioned. The main problem was the unfinished configuration of the telescope's large mirror, in accordance with a project begun in 1909 to make it the largest diameter reflecting telescope in the southern hemisphere.

    Félix Aguilar, who had been designated as one of the Observatory's auditors to adopt a definitive solution, consulted Gaviola on the subject and, in a way, this was Gaviola's start in astronomy. To break into this new field, he decided to go to work with John Strong, at the then most skilled place in telescope building, the California Institute of Technology and its associate, the Mount Wilson Observatory in California. In this place, Strong valued Gaviola's ability and soon appointed him his first assistant. Together they replaced the earlier silvering of the 60-inch and 100-inch mirrors at the Observatory with the new method introduced by Strong for aluminizing the surfaces. And it is from here that Gaviola begins a vertiginous scientific career that would lead him to receive the greatest tributes in this field.

    His innovations were notable: For the first time, he introduced the concept of quality in the analysis of surface coating material (control over aluminum deposit), through spectrometric evaluation of vaporized materials. He also developed a differential method for coating surfaces in such a way as to carry out a localized deposition that could be applied to any type of surface, whether or not it had axial symmetry.

  • 1940 to 1947 - Gaviola was appointed Director of the Cordoba Astronomical Observatory. Also during this period the Argentine Physical Association would be created.

  • 1956 to 1957 - He takes up the position of Director of the Astronomical Observatory of Córdoba and creates the Institute of Mathematics, Astronomy and Physics. Later he would develop various activities, the last one as a teacher at the Institute of Physics of Bariloche and at the National Atomic Energy Commission. Both in the genesis of these Institutions and those of CONICET and SECYT, the action of Gaviola was also fundamental.

  • 1978 - For his work in physics and optics, he was awarded the Dr. Ricardo Gans Gold Medal, awarded by the University of La Plata.

  • 1980 - He is awarded the Gold Medal of the Center for Research in Optics.

  • 1981 - His work as an astronomer was recognized by the International Astronomical Union who gave his name to asteroid 2504 discovered in Córdoba in 1967.

  • 1989 - dies at the age of 89

On a certain occasion Einstein said, more or less: if Argentina had several young people like Gaviola... and he finished the unfinished sentence with an admiring gesture.
